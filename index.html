‘The trust is absolutely broken’: Barnard initiates disciplinary processes against students after ‘unauthorized’ protest

By Maya Stahl and Sarah Huddleston Edited by Shea Vance, Esha Karam, Isabella Ramírez, Yvin Shin, and August Phillips

January 31, 2024

Esmeralda Paredes / Staff Photographer

Barnard administrators initiated disciplinary proceedings against at least 19 students they say were involved with the only pro-Palestinian protest held so far on the college’s campus, according to 10 students undergoing the process and eight longtime faculty members with knowledge of the situation.

Spectator spoke to students who received emails from administrators about their alleged involvement with the Dec. 11 protest, as well as professors who either accompanied students to their inquiry meetings or were involved in faculty-led efforts to support free expression and their students’ right to protest.

Already under scrutiny for previous unilateral policy changes related to political speech and expression, Barnard is facing further accusations of chilling speech, intimidating students who protest, and circumventing relevant parties in policy making.

The Columbia chapters of Students for Justice in Palestine and Jewish Voice for Peace announced on Dec. 9 an “emergency protest” on Barnard’s campus. The groups demanded that Barnard President Laura Rosenbury publicly call for a permanent ceasefire on behalf of the college.

The next day, Leslie Grinage, Barnard dean and vice president for campus life and student experience, and Sarah Gillman, senior vice president of strategic finance and operations, wrote in an email to the Barnard community that they had “been made aware of a protest planned on the Barnard campus for Monday afternoon. This protest has not been authorized.”

“We also want to make sure that all students are aware of Barnard’s Student Code of Conduct and that those affiliated with unauthorized protests or events are subject to the potential consequences,” the email reads.

SJP and JVP continued with their Dec. 11 protest, which drew over a hundred protesters and a group of counterprotesters to the Barnard campus. During the protest, students in the Barnard Quad hung pro-Palestinian banners outside their windows.

Students gather in Barnard Quad during the Dec. 11 protest. Photo by Chris Mendell / Columbia Daily Spectator

Weeks after the protest, students began receiving either “inquiry meeting” emails from Campus Life and Student Experience about their alleged involvement, or emails from Residential Life & Housing claiming they violated a policy that prohibits the placement of items on “window ledges.”

“We have information to suggest that you may have coordinated or participated in this event, thereby failing to comply with event policies and the directives of College officials, which is in violation with Barnard’s Code of Conduct,” one such email, obtained by Spectator, reads.

In a statement to Spectator, a Barnard College spokesperson wrote that the college “is confident in the integrity of its conduct review process, which prioritizes the dignity of our students and favors restorative and educational resolutions.”

Students expressed confusion to Spectator as to how administrators identified them. Two email recipients said that they did not attend the Dec. 11 protest at all.

“I think the students feel also confused about why they in particular were selected for hearings,” Frederick Neuhouser, GSAS ’80, GSAS ’88, a professor of German and philosophy who accompanied one student to their inquiry meeting, said. “And it feels to the students a little creepy, like, ‘Are we being spied on?’ It feels a little bit like Big Brother’s watching.”

‘Singled out’

Maryam Iqbal, BC ’27, met Rosenbury and Grinage in October at a dinner to which she and other Arab and Muslim students were invited following student outcry over the administrative response to the Israel-Hamas war. At the dinner, Iqbal mentioned to Grinage and Rosenbury that she was a member of SJP.

“[That] was my first mistake,” Iqbal said. “Because now admin latches on to whoever they can get and makes an example out of them.”

The inquiry meeting email sent out to students says that they “may have coordinated or participated” in the Dec. 11 unauthorized protest, but does not clarify which action each student is accused of.

An incident report obtained by Spectator states that “the student(s) of concern were positively identified either by onsite administrative personnel or via social media images.”

Cierra Martinez, BC ’24, received an email requesting an inquiry meeting but said she did not attend the Dec. 11 protest. She was working at her on-campus job at the time of the protest and was confused upon waking up to an inquiry meeting letter on Jan. 18, she said.

A Barnard junior, who spoke to Spectator under the condition of anonymity citing concerns of academic or financial consequences, also received an inquiry meeting request but said she did not attend the protest—she was only “passing through” while on a video call with her mom.

“I literally was just walking by and I just stopped and stared for a couple seconds to show my mom, and then I walked away,” she said.

The student said that she was walking with her two roommates at the time, but they did not receive similar inquiry meeting emails.

During her inquiry meeting, the student said she asked how administrators identified her, and the investigator conducting the meeting responded that a staff member reported her. The student said she told the investigator that she felt “singled out or targeted.”

The investigator told her that she could not name the staff member and to file a Title IX incident report if she felt targeted, the student said. The student said she responded that she couldn’t file a report because she did not know the staff member’s name.

“I literally am so paranoid walking through campus,” the student said. “I literally took my name tag off my door. Like, I’ve been so paranoid and just scared. … I don’t even like to raise my hand anymore in class when it comes to certain topics.”

Izzy Lapidus, BC ’24, said that they had not seen Grinage’s Dec. 10 email and did not know “if the protest was authorized or unauthorized.” Lapidus said she attended the Dec. 11 protest after seeing “students starting to march and gather in the Barnard Quad.”

“It was an absolute shock to me when, on the second day of classes, I got an email with the subject line ‘inquiry meeting,’” Lapidus said.

Tomisin Fasosin, BC ’25, said that the investigator told her during her inquiry meeting that she was identified by an administrator who saw her at the Dec. 11 protest. Fasosin said that at the meeting, she was asked if she knew that “it was ‘wrong’ to be” at the protest. She was told that if she were to attend another “unauthorized event,” she would “go straight into a conduct meeting,” Fasosin said.

Martinez said that even though she didn’t attend the protest, she was concerned that the inquiry meetings could put students at risk of losing their financial aid.

“I’m on financial aid. And like, literally every year it’s like a battle to get enough financial aid,” Martinez said. “When my family heard about this, about me getting a hearing, they were really, really concerned because it’s like, let this be the reason why I can’t get my degree in the last semester.”

The policies

Nine students who spoke to Spectator were told that they were in violation of the Barnard College campus events and approval policy, which outlines rules for organizing and gaining administrative approval to host events—including demonstrations and protests—on Barnard’s campus. The policy requires students and faculty to submit requests for public demonstrations, categorized as “Events with Resources,” at least “28 calendar days prior to the proposed Event date.”

“Barnard is committed to ensuring that students have access to events and speakers that represent a diverse range of thought, and the time restrictions are in place to ensure availability of resources, including the staffing of our community safety team,”a Barnard College spokesperson wrote in a statement to Spectator.

The event policy was put into effect on Nov. 2, according to a version of the policy published via a Google document on the Information for Protesters webpage, and drew concerns from faculty who told Spectator that Barnard administration unilaterally changed the policies without their consultation.

“These time restrictions have long been in place at Barnard,” the spokesperson wrote. “Barnard is working with our community to review and improve our existing policies, with a particular focus on ensuring that we continue to protect freedom of expression and the safety and well-being of our students.”

In late October, Barnard took down a “Solidarity with Palestine” statement from the women’s, gender, and sexuality studies departmental web page before altering the college’s political activity policy in November and implementing new administrative controls over what faculty, students, and staff can say using college resources.

The New York Civil Liberties Union sent a letter in late December to Rosenbury in response to the departmental website policy changes, writing that the “requirement imposes a ‘prior restraint’ in violation of fundamental free speech principles and in a manner incompatible with a sound understanding of ‘academic freedom.’”

Barnard’s policy for development of Barnard College administrative policies—colloquially referred to among faculty as Barnard’s “policy on policies”—states that the vice president for the relevant “area of the College” must consult “key stakeholders” during the drafting process and consider whether “affected constituencies” are aware of the policy draft before it’s approved.

María Rivera Maulucci, BC ’88, TC ’05, a professor of education and chair of the college’s faculty governance and procedures committee, defined the “affected constituencies” and “key stakeholders” as faculty, staff, and students, as they organize events. Members of these groups were not consulted in drafting and approving the updated event policy, according to Maulucci.

“The procedure for making new policy and for changing policies is supposed to begin with consulting the constituencies that might be affected by the policy,” Maulucci said. “I don’t have any evidence that constituents were consulted with in the crafting of the new policy.”

The Barnard College spokesperson wrote that the college’s policies “are created with the intent of fostering an environment where all members of our community can safely pursue their scholarship, share their ideas, and learn, free from disruption, harassment, or discrimination.”

“In recent months, we reviewed and updated our policies for events and on-campus demonstrations, following the procedures outlined in the Barnard Policy on Policies,” the spokesperson wrote.

Spectator spoke to two students who received emails from Residential Life requiring that they attend an “administrative conduct meeting” concerning their alleged violation of a Residential Life rule regarding “Defenestration or items placed on window ledges.”

In the email, Barnard cited a New York City ordinance prohibiting “placing articles of any kind … outside the window, as well as throwing or projecting anything out of a window.” However, New York City code states that hanging “flags, banners or pennants” is “permitted without limitation” in “community facilities,” which Barnard dormitories are zoned under.

Jeffrey Manning, director of Residential Life, emailed another student that “It has been and will remain the policy of the College that throwing, projecting, or hanging items out of a residence hall window is prohibited.” Manning acknowledged that the ordinance cited in the previous email was outdated.

“Barnard remains in steadfast support of students’ right to free expression,” the Barnard College spokesperson wrote, reiterating the residential policy that Manning cited.

“This is for the physical safety of those in the proximity of our buildings, including on adjacent city sidewalks,” the spokesperson wrote. “This is regardless of the content depicted or conveyed by the item(s). The application of such policies and procedures is content-neutral and not directed at any political ideology, national origin, race, or shared ancestry.”

Katherine Franke, BC ’81, a professor of law, said that she believes Barnard created this new rule regarding students’ right to hang flags outside their dorm windows and applied it retroactively against students. She said Barnard itself violates this new rule because it hangs Barnard College flags outside of Barnard Hall.

“There was no violation that said that at the time, December of last year, so what they’re doing is they’re making up a new rule and putting it into the Residential Life handbook,” Franke said. “And then applying it retroactively to students who they, I guess, are alleging that they hung flags outside of their windows.”

The meetings

Barnard students may have a “​​support person” accompany them to their inquiry meetings or hearings for alleged violations of the Barnard Student Code of Conduct. The person “must be a member of the College community, not otherwise involved in the incident, and not a practicing attorney,” the code reads.

Columbia students, however—who undergo a separate disciplinary process and appear before a different judicial body—may have an attorney present during a hearing for alleged violations of the Rules of University Conduct.

The Residential Life student guide does not specify whether students can bring a support person to their meetings. A Barnard senior, who spoke to Spectator under the condition of anonymity citing fear of future residential probation, said they were not allowed to bring a faculty member to accompany them during their Residential Life hearing this semester.

Debbie Becher, an associate professor of sociology, said that “the trust is absolutely broken” between students and the administration.

“The administration could have had conversations with these students without the threat, without a gun to their head,” Becher said. “And that’s not what they do.”

Becher accompanied two students to their respective inquiry meetings. She said many students receiving these inquiry meeting emails are “vulnerable.”

“These particular meetings seem to be targeting the most vulnerable students, students of color, first-gen students, low-income students, not only, but certainly a great number of them are wrapped up in this,” Becher said.

Both Nadia Abu El-Haj, a professor of anthropology, and Franke accompanied a student to their Residential Life administrative conduct meeting.

“The students are really not feeling safe in these meetings,” Franke said. “We won’t say anything, but we want to come in and [Residential Life] dug in and they said, ‘It is our practice. We do not allow it, you have to leave.”

Abu El-Haj said that the hearings establish a “confrontational relationship” between students and the administration.

“It feels like this whole process is really about intimidation. That is certainly how the students are experiencing it,” Abu El-Haj said.

Abu El-Haj emphasized that she is in steadfast support of all speech on college campuses and that the restriction of any speech “is not acceptable.” She said that the issue at hand is the fact that inquiry meetings and administrative pursuit of disciplinary action against students are “not content-neutral enforcement.”

“I really don’t care what these students’ politics are,” Abu El-Haj said. “I don’t understand the way the administrations have been responding. It seems overly disciplinary, it seems overly cruel. And are we really not going to allow civil protests on campuses?”

According to the Barnard Student Code of Conduct, Barnard students found guilty of a Student Code of Conduct violation are subject to sanctions including probation, loss of privileges, suspension, and expulsion. Students can appeal imposed sanctions within five business days of the decision.

All inquiry meetings were postponed on Jan. 22 without a clear reason, according to Najam Haider, chair of the religion department. The next day, inquiry meetings proceeded.

Barnard faculty sent a letter on Jan. 19 to Rosenbury, Grinage, and Barnard Provost and Dean of Faculty Linda Bell calling for the cancellation of all planned disciplinary action concerning the Dec. 11 unauthorized protest. As of Jan. 21, 106 Barnard faculty had signed onto the letter.

“We write concerning the disciplinary proceedings against nearly two dozen Barnard students, proceedings which accuse them of attending and, in some cases, organizing an ‘unauthorized protest,’” the letter reads. “While the summonses issued to students may be supported by specific college rules, the college’s unprecedented decision to enforce these rules in a way that adversely affects students with one particular set of political beliefs undermines any collective sense of transparency, process, procedure, or fairness.”

Rosenbury responded to the faculty letter on Jan. 25, writing that she has “the utmost confidence in the integrity of our conduct process as administered by Dean Grinage, whose team is responsible for the process.”

“The process is fair and unbiased, and Dean Grinage and her team consistently respect the individual dignity of each student and the educational mission of the College as a whole,” Rosenbury wrote. “To that end, our process favors informal educational resolutions whenever possible.”

Rosenbury did not respond to faculty calls for more information about the inquiry meetings and wrote that Barnard has enacted its disciplinary process in the past, even though she can “certainly understand how these recent proceedings may seem unprecedented.”

Taylor Carman, a professor of philosophy, said he’s “never experienced anything like what’s going on now” in his three decades at Barnard.

“I think there’s a level of anxiety and anger and, frankly, distrust between administration and faculty,” Carman said.

With growing divisions between Barnard’s administration and its faculty and students, Becher voiced concerns about the effects of the administration’s clamping down on campus speech and expression.

“I’m worried either that this movement towards repression of speech is going to work and … many of us who care to be otherwise will leave,” Becher said. “And the school will be populated by students and faculty administrators who are willing to be silent, or don’t have any other choice, need to keep their job, and are willing to be silent under this kind of regime.”

‘Symptom of this larger crisis’

Rosenbury will be inaugurated as Barnard’s ninth president on Friday, ushering in a new era of leadership at the college and symbolically marking the turbulent beginning of Barnard’s next chapter. Three pro-Palestinian student groups—Columbia University Apartheid Divest, SJP, and JVP—called on students to “boycott” Friday’s pomp and circumstance in a Jan. 26 Instagram post.

The groups demanded that Rosenbury “stop her blatant censorship of both faculty and students, which has caused Barnard to be put on watch by the NYCLU for violation of academic freedom.”

Six pro-Palestinian organizations, including the national chapter of SJP, announced on Monday an “All Out for Palestine at Columbia University” protest on the day of Rosenbury’s inauguration in response to an alleged chemical spraying at a Jan. 19 demonstration on Low Steps.

Franke, who attended Barnard in the late ’70s and early ’80s, said that the administration tolerated and allowed protests when she was an undergraduate as aligning with the university’s purpose to promote debate and free speech.

“They’re making enormous mistakes in how they’re disciplining students and they ought to just decide to dismiss all of these complaints against the students from last semester for all of the irregularities in their enforcement,” Franke said. “As a Barnard alum, I’m actually quite shocked and disappointed in Barnard’s administration and how they’re handling this.”

Janet Jakobsen, a professor of women’s, gender, and sexuality studies, has taught at Barnard for 23 years and said the issue is a “symptom of this larger crisis in higher education.”

“If we are an institution that is dedicated to free inquiry, to academic work, to the production of new knowledge, then we should be interested in supporting freedom of expression, basic First Amendment rights, as well as academic freedom,” Jakobsen said.

Carman emphasized that the faculty “maintain a very robust defense in ideals of freedom of inquiry and freedom of expression and academic freedom.”

“I think the whole point of having a college and a university is for the sake of the pursuit of knowledge, unfettered pursuit of knowledge,” Carman said. “And that means robust communication, and only limiting speech when it’s absolutely necessary.”

Faculty will meet with Rosenbury during their regularly scheduled faculty meeting on Feb. 5, where they plan on addressing the inquiry meetings—which are ongoing.

Two of the four students who attended their inquiry meetings and spoke to Spectator said they were let off with a verbal warning.

The students said they were told that if they attend another unauthorized protest or have another accusation filed against them, they could be called to a formal conduct meeting, the next step in the disciplinary process that could lead to more severe consequences.
